Direction

Why this professional direction

Before building this portfolio I researched what product management and data analytics roles actually look like in practice — not just job titles, but what the day-to-day work involves, what tools matter, and what makes someone credible at an early career stage.

What I found: the most valuable IS professionals aren't purely technical or purely strategic — they're the ones who can move between both. A product manager who can't read data is flying blind. A data analyst who can't communicate findings to non-technical stakeholders isn't generating value. The roles I'm targeting — PM, business analyst, data strategy — all sit at that intersection.

My internship experience confirms this. At GE Vernova, the LLM project required understanding both the technical architecture (how to structure company data for retrieval, how to integrate with a language model) and the organizational problem (what questions do employees actually need answered, how do we get adoption). At Data Nova, the analytics work was only valuable when it translated into a stakeholder presentation that drove a decision.

That's the professional I'm building toward — someone who understands systems deeply enough to build them, and communicates clearly enough to make them matter.

AI Workflow

How I used AI to build this

This portfolio was built using a spec-driven development process with Claude (Anthropic) as the primary AI tool. The process wasn't "ask AI to make a website" — it was a structured workflow where I made the decisions and used AI to execute them faster and better than I could alone.

How the process worked

I started by defining the professional direction I wanted to communicate — IS / product management / data analytics — before touching any code. Then I provided Claude with my resume, the assignment requirements, and specific content decisions (what to include, what to cut, how to frame each role). Claude generated the HTML/CSS structure and I reviewed, revised, and directed each iteration.

What I kept vs. changed

The GE Vernova description went through multiple revisions — the first version was too generic ("collaborated with teams"). I pushed back and provided the specific detail about the LLM project, which made it significantly stronger. I also cut the Live Long & Profit section entirely because it no longer represented where I'm headed professionally. Every section reflects a deliberate choice, not just AI output.

What this process taught me

Spec-driven development is essentially the same skill as requirements gathering in business analysis — you have to know what you want before you can direct someone (or something) to build it. The quality of the output is directly proportional to the quality of the specification. That's true whether you're managing an intern, briefing a developer, or prompting an AI model.
